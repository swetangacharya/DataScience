what is Latent Variable?

We introduce latent (hidden) variables in Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) to simplify complex data distributions and make parameter estimation computationally feasible. They represent unobserved, underlying factors that explain the patterns in our data. 
When data is generated by overlapping distributions (e.g., Gaussian Mixture Models or clusters), computing the log-likelihood involves a logarithm of a sum:  
$log \ p(X)= log \sum_z p(X,Z)$  , i.e., it is the joint probability between R.V X and latent variable Z in GMM, then take sum over all the Z (discrete or random).   

This prevents direct differentiation, making standard MLE intractable. By introducing a latent variable  (the missing information, such as the specific cluster a data point belongs to), we transition to the complete-data likelihood function:      
$log \sum_z p(X,Z)$   
This allows us to iteratively optimize parameters using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) Algorithm, vastly simplifying the math.    
In high-dimensional datasets, latent variables allow us to compress data into fewer underlying dimensions (e.g., Principal Component Analysis or Factor Analysis). This drastically reduces noise, handles measurement errors, and uncovers compact, interpretable structures without losing the core information of the original dataset.    

The classic example of using a latent variable is a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) used for customer segmentation or clustering. Imagine you own a clothing store and collect data on customer heights. If you plot the data, you see two distinct peaks because your customers are a mix of adults and children.
Here is how the data breaks down:   
Observed Variable (X): The height of each customer (e.g. 130 cm,170 cm ). You can easily measure this. 
Latent Variabl (Z): The demographic group(Adult vs Child). you only recorded the heights, not the ages, so this group identity is hidden.

1. Data without latent variable: if we look only at the observed heights (X), the data looks like complex, two-humped distribution. Trying to fit as a Gaussian Curve to this data fails completely.   
2. Introduce Latent Variable (Z): Z=1 if customer is child, Z=2 if customer is adult.
3. How Z simplifies the MLE: if we knew Z for every customer then MLE would be easy. 
-> separate children from adultsbased on Z.   
-> calculate avg height and variance for children's group(Z=1)and same for adult's group (Z=2)

because Z is latent(unknown), the Expectation Maximization(EM) algorithm guesses for you. it calculates the probability that a130 cm person belongs to child cluster(Z=1) versus the adult cluster(Z=2), and uses those guesses to perfectly map both bell curves.   

Let us assume that our data come from two groups (k= 2 groups) $z_i \in {1,2}$. It follows multinomial distribution governed by mixing proportions $\pi_k$   
The Conditional Distribution:  
if we know a person belongs to group k (i.e., $z_i=k$), then their height follows a specific normal distribution with mean $\mu_k$ and variance ${\sigma_k}^2$   

$P(x_i|z_i=k)= N(x_i|\mu_k,{\sigma_k}^2)= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma_k^2}} exp(-\frac{(x_i-\mu_k)^2}{2 \sigma_k^2})$

The joint and Marginal Distributions:  
The joint probability of observing a height $x_i$ and its hidden group $z_i$ is:  
$P(x_i,z_i=k) = P(z_i=k)P(x_i|z_i=k)= \pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_i|\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)$  
Because $x_i is unobserved, we marginalize(sum) over all possible states of $Z$ to find the probability of the observed data alone:

$P(x_i)= \sum_{k=1}^{2} \pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_i|\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)$  

Now, to find the parameter $\theta={\pi_k,\mu_k,\sigma_k^2}$, we maximize log-likelihood of the observed dataset X:   
$log \ L(\theta|X)= \sum_{i=1}^N log \left( \sum_{k=1}^2 \pi_k đť“ť(x_i|\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)\right)$   

The sum inside the logarithm makes direct analytical differentiation impossible. Instead, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm uses the posterior probability of the latent variable (called responsibility $\gamma_{ik}$)   

$\gamma_{ik}=P(z_i=k|x_i)= \left( \frac{\pi_k đť“ť(x_i|\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)}{\sum_{j=1}^2 \pi_k đť“ť(x_i|\mu_j,\sigma_j^2)}  \right)$

This step calculates exactly how likely a specific height measurement belongs to the child cluster versus the adult cluster.    

Now, for the maximization step, we'll find new $\mu_k$ for iteration t+1 ...   

$\mu_k^{t+1} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_{ik}x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_{ik}}$
then we can find new variance...
$\sigma_k^{t+1}= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_{ik} (x_i-\mu_k^{t+1})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_{ik}}$
and $\pi_k^{t+1}= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_{ik}}{N}$    

Let us take an example of hight(in cm) of few persons recorded as $X={160,170,180}$   
suppose at t=0, we have initial guesses are:  
cluster 1: $\pi_1=0.5,\mu_1=162, \sigma_1^2=25$   
cluster 2: $\pi_1=0.5,\mu_1=178, \sigma_1^2=25$   

step 1: E-Step(Compute responsibilities)  
$x_1=160, P(x_1|z=1)= \frac{1}{5 \sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-\frac{(160-162)^2}{2(25)}}=0.735$,  
$x_1=160, P(x_1|z=2)= \frac{1}{5 \sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-\frac{(160-178)^2}{2(25)}}=0.0001$,   
$\frac {(0.5 * 0.0735)}{(0.5*0.0735)+(0.5*0.0001)}=0.999$
and $\gamma_{1,2}=1-0.999=0.001$  

same way for $x_2=170$, responsibilities $\gamma_{2,1}=0.500 , \gamma_{2,2}=0.500 $
same way for $x_2=170$, responsibilities $\gamma_{3,1}=0.001 , \gamma_{3,2}=0.999 $

Step 2: M-Step (Update Parameters)  
compute $\mu_1^{(1)}$ for cluster 1:      
$\sum \gamma_{i,1}=0.999+0.500+0.001=1.500$, so new mean value is
